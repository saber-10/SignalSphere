-----------------------------------------------
SECTION 1: MOTIVATION — FROM THE DTFT TO A FINITE, COMPUTABLE TRANSFORM
-----------------------------------------------

THE PROBLEM WITH THE DTFT FOR COMPUTATION:
The DTFT X(e^(j*omega)), as defined in fourier_analysis.txt, is a
function of the CONTINUOUS variable omega and, for an infinite-length
sequence, requires summing infinitely many terms. Neither property is
directly implementable on a digital computer: a computer can only
store and operate on a FINITE number of samples of the frequency axis,
and can only sum a FINITE number of terms.

DEFINITION — THE DFT AS SAMPLES OF THE DTFT:
For a FINITE-LENGTH sequence x[n] defined for n = 0, 1, ..., N-1 (zero
elsewhere), the N-POINT DISCRETE FOURIER TRANSFORM (DFT) is defined as
the DTFT of x[n] evaluated at exactly N equally spaced frequencies
around the unit circle:

        X[k] = X(e^(j*omega)) |_{omega = 2*pi*k/N}
             = Sum from n = 0 to N-1 of x[n] * exp(-j*2*pi*k*n/N)
                                            for k = 0, 1, ..., N-1

This IS the DFT ANALYSIS EQUATION. The frequency spacing between
adjacent DFT samples is 2*pi/N (in normalized DT angular frequency),
so a LARGER N samples the underlying DTFT MORE FINELY (denser
frequency resolution), though it does not, by itself, change the
underlying continuous DTFT curve being sampled.

DFT SYNTHESIS EQUATION (Inverse DFT, IDFT):
        x[n] = (1/N) * Sum from k = 0 to N-1 of
               X[k] * exp(j*2*pi*k*n/N)                for n = 0,...,N-1

NOTATIONAL SHORTHAND — THE TWIDDLE FACTOR:
Define W_N = exp(-j*2*pi/N) (the "N-th root of unity" twiddle factor).
Then the DFT pair can be written compactly as:

        X[k] = Sum_{n=0}^{N-1} x[n] * W_N^(n*k)
        x[n] = (1/N) * Sum_{k=0}^{N-1} X[k] * W_N^(-n*k)

-----------------------------------------------
SECTION 2: THE DFT AS AN IMPLICITLY PERIODIC (CIRCULAR) TRANSFORM
-----------------------------------------------

CRITICAL PROPERTY — IMPLICIT PERIODICITY:
Although the DFT is defined using only N samples of x[n] (n=0,...,N-1)
and produces only N samples of X[k] (k=0,...,N-1), BOTH the analysis
and synthesis equations are algebraically identical to evaluating a
PERIODIC extension of x[n] (period N) and X[k] (period N), because
exp(j*2*pi*k*n/N) is periodic in both k and n with period N. As a
direct consequence:

        X[k + N] = X[k]      for all integers k
        x[n + N] = x[n]      for all integers n

(when x[n] and X[k] are extended beyond their original defining
ranges using the DFT formulas themselves). This is why DFT-based
operations such as shifting and convolution are inherently CIRCULAR
(modulo-N) rather than linear — see Section 4.

RELATIONSHIP TO THE DTFS:
The DFT is mathematically identical in form to the DTFS analysis/
synthesis equations (see fourier_series.txt, Section 2), with the sign
convention on the exponent and normalization differing only by which
equation carries the 1/N factor. The key conceptual distinction is
one of INTERPRETATION: the DTFS is used for signals that are
GENUINELY, PHYSICALLY periodic, whereas the DFT is used as a
computational TOOL applied to a FINITE-LENGTH (often aperiodic)
sequence, with periodicity arising only as an artifact of the discrete
sampling of its DTFT.

-----------------------------------------------
SECTION 3: CORE PROPERTIES OF THE DFT
-----------------------------------------------

All sequences below are assumed length N (or zero-padded to length N),
and all shifts/index arithmetic are understood MODULO N (circular),
per Section 2.

--- 3.1 LINEARITY ---

        a*x_1[n] + b*x_2[n]   <--->   a*X_1[k] + b*X_2[k]

--- 3.2 CIRCULAR TIME SHIFT ---

        x[(n - n_0) mod N]   <--->   X[k] * exp(-j*2*pi*k*n_0/N)

Only the PHASE of X[k] is affected; |X[k]| is unchanged. This is the
circular (finite-length) analog of the DTFT time-shift property.

--- 3.3 CIRCULAR CONVOLUTION (THE DFT'S CONVOLUTION PROPERTY) ---

Define the N-POINT CIRCULAR CONVOLUTION of two length-N sequences as:

        x_1[n] (circleX) x_2[n] = Sum_{m=0}^{N-1}
                                   x_1[m] * x_2[(n - m) mod N]

Then:
        x_1[n] (circleX) x_2[n]   <--->   X_1[k] * X_2[k]

This is the DFT analog of the ordinary (linear) convolution property
of the DTFT/CTFT (fourier_analysis.txt, Section 3.6): multiplication
of DFTs corresponds to CIRCULAR, not linear, convolution of the
time-domain sequences.

--- 3.4 RELATIONSHIP BETWEEN CIRCULAR AND LINEAR CONVOLUTION ---

If x_1[n] has length L_1 and x_2[n] has length L_2, their ORDINARY
(linear) convolution has length L_1 + L_2 - 1 (see
convolution_and_impulse_response.txt, Section 2.5). The N-point
circular convolution of x_1 and x_2 EQUALS their linear convolution
if and only if both sequences are ZERO-PADDED to a common length:

        N >= L_1 + L_2 - 1

If this condition is not met, the circular convolution result is the
linear convolution result with "wrap-around" TIME-ALIASING: samples
that would have fallen at indices >= N wrap around and add into the
low-index samples of the result.

--- 3.5 MULTIPLICATION IN TIME (DUAL OF 3.3) ---

        x_1[n] * x_2[n] [pointwise product]   <--->
              (1/N) * [X_1[k] (circleX) X_2[k]]
              (circular convolution in the DFT domain)

--- 3.6 CONJUGATE SYMMETRY (REAL-VALUED SEQUENCES) ---

If x[n] is real-valued, then:

        X[(N - k) mod N] = X[k]*

so |X[k]| is even (with respect to the circular index) and angle(X[k])
is odd. Consequently, for a real length-N sequence, only the DFT bins
k = 0, 1, ..., floor(N/2) contain independent information; the
remaining bins are determined by conjugate symmetry. This is the
computational basis for "real FFT" routines that avoid computing (or
storing) redundant bins.

--- 3.7 PARSEVAL'S RELATION FOR THE DFT ---

        Sum_{n=0}^{N-1} |x[n]|^2  =  (1/N) * Sum_{k=0}^{N-1} |X[k]|^2

-----------------------------------------------
SECTION 4: ZERO-PADDING AND FREQUENCY RESOLUTION
-----------------------------------------------

EFFECT OF ZERO-PADDING:
Appending zeros to a finite-length sequence before computing the DFT
(increasing N without adding any new signal information) does NOT
change the underlying continuous DTFT of the (still finite-support)
sequence; it only causes the DFT to sample that SAME underlying DTFT
curve MORE DENSELY (more, closer-spaced frequency samples). This is
often called "interpolating" the spectrum, though it is important to
note zero-padding provides no new spectral RESOLUTION in the sense of
distinguishing two closely spaced true sinusoidal components — it only
produces a smoother-looking, more finely sampled picture of the
resolution the original (unpadded) data actually supports.

TRUE FREQUENCY RESOLUTION:
Genuine frequency resolution (the ability to distinguish two closely
spaced sinusoids) is fundamentally limited by the ORIGINAL signal
duration/length (equivalently, the width of the DTFT's main-lobe for
a finite observation window), not by the DFT length N chosen for
computing it. For a length-L observation window, the DTFT main-lobe
width (and hence the practical frequency resolution) is on the order
of 2*pi/L radians/sample.

-----------------------------------------------
SECTION 5: THE FAST FOURIER TRANSFORM (FFT)
-----------------------------------------------

MOTIVATION — COMPUTATIONAL COST:
Computed directly from its definition, an N-point DFT requires, for
each of the N output values X[k], a sum of N complex multiply-add
terms, giving a total computational cost that grows as O(N^2) complex
multiplications.

THE FFT AS A FAST ALGORITHM (NOT A DIFFERENT TRANSFORM):
The FAST FOURIER TRANSFORM (FFT) is not a different transform from the
DFT — it produces EXACTLY the same numerical output X[k] as the direct
DFT summation — but is instead a family of algorithms that compute
that same output far more efficiently, with computational cost
O(N*log2(N)) instead of O(N^2), by recursively exploiting the
symmetry and periodicity properties of the twiddle factors W_N^(nk).

RADIX-2 DECIMATION-IN-TIME (DIT) FFT — CORE IDEA:
For N a power of 2, the N-point DFT sum is split into two (N/2)-point
DFT sums, one over the EVEN-indexed input samples and one over the
ODD-indexed input samples:

        X[k] = Sum_{n even} x[n]*W_N^(nk) + Sum_{n odd} x[n]*W_N^(nk)
             = E[k] + W_N^k * O[k]

where E[k] and O[k] are themselves (N/2)-point DFTs of the even- and
odd-indexed subsequences (each periodic in k with period N/2), and
W_N^k is the twiddle factor for stage combination. Because E[k] and
O[k] each have period N/2 in k, the two (N/2)-point DFTs, once
computed, can be reused (via this "butterfly" combination) to
construct all N values of X[k], not just N/2 of them:

        X[k]       = E[k] + W_N^k * O[k]          for k = 0,...,N/2-1
        X[k + N/2] = E[k] - W_N^k * O[k]          for k = 0,...,N/2-1

RECURSIVE COMPLEXITY ARGUMENT:
Applying this even/odd splitting RECURSIVELY (splitting each (N/2)-
point DFT into two (N/4)-point DFTs, and so on, down to trivial
1-point DFTs) produces log2(N) stages, each stage requiring O(N)
butterfly operations, for the stated overall O(N*log2(N)) complexity.

PRACTICAL IMPLICATION: because the FFT computes circular convolution
(via Section 3.3-3.4) far more cheaply than direct time-domain
convolution for large sequence lengths, "FAST CONVOLUTION" methods
(e.g., the OVERLAP-ADD and OVERLAP-SAVE block-convolution techniques)
use zero-padded FFTs together with the linear/circular equivalence
condition of Section 3.4 to compute LINEAR convolutions of long
sequences efficiently.

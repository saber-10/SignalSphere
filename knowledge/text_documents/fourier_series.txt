-----------------------------------------------
SECTION 1: CONTINUOUS-TIME FOURIER SERIES (CTFS)
-----------------------------------------------

SETUP:
The CTFS applies only to PERIODIC continuous-time signals. Let x(t) be
periodic with fundamental period T and fundamental angular frequency:

        omega_0 = 2*pi / T

SYNTHESIS EQUATION (Forward direction — building x(t) from coefficients):
Provided x(t) satisfies the Dirichlet conditions (see below), it can be
represented exactly as a weighted sum of harmonically related complex
exponentials:

        x(t) = Sum from k = -infinity to infinity of
               a_k * exp(j*k*omega_0*t)

where a_k are the FOURIER SERIES COEFFICIENTS and k indexes the k-th
HARMONIC (k=0 is the DC term, k=+/-1 is the fundamental, k=+/-2 is the
second harmonic, and so on).

ANALYSIS EQUATION (Recovering coefficients from x(t)):
The coefficients are extracted by integrating over any single period:

        a_k = (1/T) * Integral over one period T of
              x(t) * exp(-j*k*omega_0*t) dt

DIRICHLET CONDITIONS (sufficient conditions for CTFS convergence):
A periodic signal x(t) has a valid CTFS representation if, over one
period, ALL of the following hold:
   (1) x(t) is absolutely integrable: Integral over one period of
       |x(t)| dt < infinity.
   (2) x(t) has a FINITE number of maxima and minima within any single
       period (bounded variation).
   (3) x(t) has a FINITE number of discontinuities within any single
       period, and each such discontinuity is finite (a finite jump).
At a jump discontinuity, the CTFS synthesis sum converges to the
AVERAGE of the left-hand and right-hand limits of x(t) at that point
(this is the same GIBBS PHENOMENON behavior seen when truncating the
series to a finite number of harmonics: a fixed-height overshoot
persists near discontinuities regardless of how many harmonics are
included, though the overshoot narrows toward the discontinuity as
more terms are added).

RELATION TO POWER (PARSEVAL'S THEOREM FOR CTFS):
Since periodic signals are power signals (see
signals_and_systems_basics.txt, Section 3), the average power of x(t)
over one period equals the sum of the powers contained in each
harmonic:

        P_infinity = (1/T) * Integral over one period of |x(t)|^2 dt
                   = Sum from k = -infinity to infinity of |a_k|^2

This states that total average power is conserved between the time
domain and the (discrete) frequency domain of harmonic coefficients.

-----------------------------------------------
SECTION 2: DISCRETE-TIME FOURIER SERIES (DTFS)
-----------------------------------------------

SETUP:
The DTFS applies to PERIODIC discrete-time signals. Let x[n] be
periodic with fundamental period N and fundamental angular frequency:

        omega_0 = 2*pi / N

CRITICAL DIFFERENCE FROM CTFS — FINITE NUMBER OF HARMONICS:
Because exp(j*k*omega_0*n) is itself periodic IN k with period N (a
direct consequence of the DT complex exponential periodicity condition
discussed in signals_and_systems_basics.txt, Section 2), there are
only N DISTINCT harmonics available in discrete time (k = 0, 1, ...,
N-1, or any other set of N consecutive integers). This is the single
most important structural difference between the CTFS (infinitely many
distinct harmonics) and the DTFS (exactly N distinct harmonics).

SYNTHESIS EQUATION:
        x[n] = Sum over any N consecutive values of k of
               a_k * exp(j*k*omega_0*n)
             = (1/N)*Sum_{k=<N>} ... [see analysis equation below for
               the coefficient definition; the synthesis sum itself
               carries no 1/N factor]

ANALYSIS EQUATION:
        a_k = (1/N) * Sum over any N consecutive values of n of
              x[n] * exp(-j*k*omega_0*n)

Both the synthesis sum (over k) and the analysis sum (over n) need
only run over N consecutive integers (commonly n = 0,...,N-1 and
k = 0,...,N-1), since both x[n] and a_k are periodic sequences with
period N:

        a_{k+N} = a_k        for all integers k

CONVERGENCE:
Unlike the CTFS, the DTFS involves only a FINITE sum (N terms), so no
Dirichlet-type convergence conditions are required — the DTFS exists
for every periodic DT sequence, with exact (not merely mean-square or
pointwise-average) equality between x[n] and its synthesis sum at
every n, including at any "discontinuity" (DT sequences have no jump
discontinuities in the CT sense, since they are only ever defined at
integers).

PARSEVAL'S THEOREM FOR DTFS:
        P_infinity = (1/N) * Sum over one period N of |x[n]|^2
                   = Sum over one period N of |a_k|^2

-----------------------------------------------
SECTION 3: CORE PROPERTIES OF THE FOURIER SERIES
-----------------------------------------------

Notation: x(t) <---FS,omega_0---> a_k denotes a CTFS pair with
fundamental frequency omega_0; x[n] <---FS,N---> a_k denotes a DTFS
pair with fundamental period N. Both x_1, x_2 (or a_k, b_k) are assumed
to share the same fundamental period/frequency in each property below.

--- 3.1 LINEARITY ---

CTFS:    A*x_1(t) + B*x_2(t)  <--->  A*a_k + B*b_k

DTFS:    A*x_1[n] + B*x_2[n]  <--->  A*a_k + B*b_k

--- 3.2 TIME SHIFTING ---

CTFS:    x(t - t_0)  <--->  a_k * exp(-j*k*omega_0*t_0)

DTFS:    x[n - n_0]  <--->  a_k * exp(-j*k*omega_0*n_0)

A time shift multiplies each harmonic coefficient by a phase factor
that depends on the harmonic index k; the MAGNITUDE of each
coefficient, |a_k|, is unaffected.

--- 3.3 TIME REVERSAL ---

CTFS:    x(-t)  <--->  a_{-k}

DTFS:    x[-n]  <--->  a_{-k}

--- 3.4 TIME SCALING (CTFS ONLY, changes the fundamental frequency) ---

For a real scaling factor alpha > 0:
        x(alpha*t)  <--->  a_k unchanged, BUT the fundamental frequency
                            becomes alpha*omega_0 (equivalently the
                            fundamental period becomes T/alpha).
(There is no direct DTFS analog, since DT time-scaling by a
non-integer factor is not generally well-defined, and integer-factor
DT time scaling changes the sample set rather than simply rescaling a
continuous axis.)

--- 3.5 MULTIPLICATION (PERIODIC CONVOLUTION IN THE COEFFICIENT DOMAIN) ---

CTFS:    x_1(t) * x_2(t) [pointwise product]  <--->
              Sum from l = -infinity to infinity of a_l * b_{k-l}
              (discrete convolution of the two coefficient sequences)

DTFS:    x_1[n] * x_2[n] [pointwise product]  <--->
              Sum over one period N of a_l * b_{k-l}
              (PERIODIC/CIRCULAR convolution of the two length-N
              coefficient sequences, since both a_k and b_k are
              themselves periodic with period N)

--- 3.6 CONJUGATION AND CONJUGATE SYMMETRY ---

CTFS:    x*(t) [complex conjugate]  <--->  a_{-k}*

DTFS:    x*[n]  <--->  a_{-k}*

CONSEQUENCE FOR REAL SIGNALS: if x(t) (or x[n]) is REAL-VALUED, then
        a_{-k} = a_k*
(conjugate symmetry), which further implies |a_{-k}| = |a_k| (even
magnitude spectrum) and angle(a_{-k}) = -angle(a_k) (odd phase
spectrum).

-----------------------------------------------
SECTION 4: RELATIONSHIP BETWEEN FOURIER SERIES AND FOURIER TRANSFORM
-----------------------------------------------

A periodic signal does not, strictly speaking, possess an ordinary
CTFT/DTFT (it is neither absolutely integrable/summable nor finite
energy), but its CTFT/DTFT CAN be written in a generalized sense as a
train of impulses, with each impulse weighted by (2*pi times) the
corresponding Fourier series coefficient:

CTFT of a periodic x(t) with CTFS coefficients a_k:
        X(j*omega) = 2*pi * Sum from k = -infinity to infinity of
                      a_k * delta(omega - k*omega_0)

DTFT of a periodic x[n] with DTFS coefficients a_k (period N):
        X(e^(j*omega)) = 2*pi * Sum from k = -infinity to infinity of
                          a_k * delta(omega - k*omega_0 - 2*pi*l),
                          summed additionally over all integers l to
                          preserve the mandatory 2*pi periodicity of
                          the DTFT (see fourier_analysis.txt, Section 2)

This equivalence is why the CTFS/DTFS can be viewed as a special,
line-spectrum case of the more general CTFT/DTFT framework, and it is
the formal bridge that allows LTI frequency-response analysis
(developed for aperiodic signals via the CTFT/DTFT) to be applied
directly to periodic inputs as well.

-----------------------------------------------
SECTION 5: ESSENTIAL FOURIER SERIES PAIRS LOOKUP TABLE
-----------------------------------------------

--- 5.1 CONTINUOUS-TIME PAIRS (period T, fundamental freq omega_0 = 2*pi/T) ---

(1) Complex Exponential at the Fundamental:
        exp(j*omega_0*t)   <--->   a_1 = 1, all other a_k = 0

(2) Cosine:
        cos(omega_0*t)   <--->   a_1 = a_{-1} = 1/2, all other a_k = 0

(3) Sine:
        sin(omega_0*t)   <--->   a_1 = 1/(2*j) = -j/2,
                                  a_{-1} = -1/(2*j) = j/2,
                                  all other a_k = 0

(4) Periodic Rectangular (Square) Pulse Train:
    Defined over one period as x(t) = 1 for |t| < T_1, and x(t) = 0 for
    T_1 < |t| <= T/2, then repeated with period T:

        a_k = sin(k*omega_0*T_1) / (k*pi)     for k != 0
        a_0 = 2*T_1 / T

    (This is the CTFS analog of the sinc-shaped CTFT of a single
    rectangular pulse; sampling that sinc envelope at integer multiples
    of omega_0 yields the coefficients a_k, illustrating the general
    principle that CTFS coefficients of a periodically-repeated pulse
    are proportional to samples of the CTFT of one isolated pulse.)

(5) Impulse Train (period T):
        Sum over n of delta(t - n*T)   <--->   a_k = 1/T for all k

--- 5.2 DISCRETE-TIME PAIRS (period N, fundamental freq omega_0 = 2*pi/N) ---

(1) Complex Exponential at the Fundamental:
        exp(j*omega_0*n)   <--->   a_1 = 1, a_k = 0 for all other k in
                                    one period (k = 0,...,N-1)

(2) Cosine (assuming N/period chosen so the harmonic falls on an
    integer k = k_0):
        cos(k_0*omega_0*n)   <--->   a_{k_0} = a_{-k_0} = 1/2
                                      (with a_{-k_0} interpreted mod N,
                                      i.e. a_{N-k_0} = 1/2), all other
                                      a_k = 0

(3) Discrete Impulse Train (period N):
        Sum over r of delta[n - r*N]   <--->   a_k = 1/N for all k

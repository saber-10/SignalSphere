-----------------------------------------------
SECTION 1: BILATERAL (TWO-SIDED) LAPLACE TRANSFORM
-----------------------------------------------

ANALYSIS EQUATION:
The bilateral Laplace transform of a CT signal x(t) is defined as:

        X(s) = Integral from -infinity to infinity of
               x(t) * exp(-s*t) dt

where s is a complex variable, decomposed into real and imaginary
parts as:

        s = sigma + j*omega

with sigma = Real(s) and omega = Imag(s). The Laplace transform can be
interpreted as the CTFT of x(t) multiplied by a real exponential
weighting factor exp(-sigma*t); that is, X(sigma + j*omega) is the
CTFT of [x(t) * exp(-sigma*t)]. This is precisely why the Laplace
transform exists for many signals (e.g., growing exponentials) for
which the ordinary CTFT does not converge: an appropriately chosen
sigma can force convergence.

The bilateral transform always requires specification of an associated
Region of Convergence (ROC); X(s) alone, without its ROC, does not
uniquely determine x(t) (see Section 3).

-----------------------------------------------
SECTION 2: UNILATERAL (ONE-SIDED) LAPLACE TRANSFORM
-----------------------------------------------

ANALYSIS EQUATION:
The unilateral (one-sided) Laplace transform is defined with the lower
integration bound at 0- (just prior to t=0, so as to correctly capture
any impulse or discontinuity located exactly at the origin):

        X(s) = Integral from 0- to infinity of x(t) * exp(-s*t) dt

PRIMARY USE CASE:
The unilateral transform is the standard tool for solving linear
constant-coefficient differential equations WITH SPECIFIED INITIAL
CONDITIONS (e.g., circuit analysis problems where initial capacitor
voltages or inductor currents at t=0- are given). Because the
integration bound effectively ignores x(t) for t < 0, the unilateral
transform implicitly assumes (or enforces) a "causal-from-this-point"
viewpoint, and its differentiation property directly incorporates
initial conditions (see Section 5), which is precisely what is needed
to solve initial-value problems directly in the s-domain without first
solving for the homogeneous and particular solutions separately in the
time domain.

KEY DISTINCTION FROM BILATERAL TRANSFORM:
For any signal that is zero for t < 0 (i.e., already causal), the
unilateral and bilateral transforms coincide exactly. For signals that
are nonzero for t < 0, the two transforms generally differ, and the
unilateral transform technically discards information about the
signal's behavior prior to t = 0- (or, for one-sided problems, that
information is simply not part of the problem definition). Unlike the
bilateral transform, the unilateral transform does NOT require an
explicit ROC to be quoted for uniqueness in standard problems, since
it is implicitly assumed the ROC is always a right-half-plane
(consistent with assumed causality from 0- onward).

-----------------------------------------------
SECTION 3: REGION OF CONVERGENCE (ROC) — CORE RULES
-----------------------------------------------

The ROC is defined as the set of all values of s = sigma + j*omega for
which the defining integral X(s) = Integral of x(t)*exp(-s*t) dt
converges absolutely (i.e., Integral of |x(t)*exp(-s*t)| dt < infinity).

RULE 1 — STRIPS PARALLEL TO THE j*omega AXIS:
The ROC of X(s) consists of strips in the s-plane that are parallel to
the j*omega (imaginary) axis. This follows because convergence of the
Laplace integral depends only on the real part of s (i.e., on sigma =
Real(s), since |exp(-s*t)| = exp(-sigma*t) does not depend on omega);
therefore, if any point s_1 = sigma_1 + j*omega_1 lies in the ROC, then
every point with the same real part sigma_1 (for any omega) also lies
in the ROC.

RULE 2 — THE ROC CONTAINS NO POLES:
The ROC can never contain any pole of X(s), since X(s) is, by
definition, infinite (undefined) at a pole, and therefore the defining
integral cannot converge there.

RULE 3 — FINITE-DURATION SIGNALS:
If x(t) is of finite duration (i.e., x(t) = 0 outside some finite
interval [T_1, T_2]) and is absolutely integrable on that interval,
then the ROC is the ENTIRE s-PLANE, except possibly the single points
s = 0 and/or s = infinity (s=0 may be excluded if X(s) has a pole
there from a term like 1/s arising from a step-like discontinuity at
an endpoint, and the behavior at s = infinity must be separately
checked if asked about convergence "at infinity," though in standard
ROC statements covering the finite s-plane, x=0 is the only practical
exclusion to watch for).

RULE 4 — RIGHT-SIDED SIGNALS:
If x(t) is right-sided (x(t) = 0 for t < T_1, for some finite T_1),
and X(s) has at least one pole, the ROC is a right-half-plane of the
form:

        Real(s) > sigma_max

where sigma_max is the real part of the rightmost (largest real-part)
pole of X(s). The ROC extends to the right of the rightmost pole, all
the way to Real(s) = +infinity.

RULE 5 — LEFT-SIDED SIGNALS:
If x(t) is left-sided (x(t) = 0 for t > T_2, for some finite T_2), and
X(s) has at least one pole, the ROC is a left-half-plane of the form:

        Real(s) < sigma_min

where sigma_min is the real part of the leftmost (smallest real-part)
pole of X(s). The ROC extends to the left of the leftmost pole, all
the way to Real(s) = -infinity.

RULE 6 — TWO-SIDED (DOUBLY INFINITE) SIGNALS:
If x(t) is two-sided (neither right-sided nor left-sided, extending
indefinitely in both directions), the ROC, if it exists at all, is a
VERTICAL STRIP in the s-plane:

        sigma_1 < Real(s) < sigma_2

bounded on both sides by poles of X(s) (specifically, the ROC strip
lies between two poles, with no other poles inside the strip; the
signal can be decomposed into a right-sided part contributing the
left boundary sigma_1 and a left-sided part contributing the right
boundary sigma_2). It is possible for a two-sided signal to have NO
valid ROC at all, in which case its bilateral Laplace transform does
not exist.

-----------------------------------------------
SECTION 4: LTI SYSTEM ANALYSIS USING THE LAPLACE TRANSFORM
-----------------------------------------------

TRANSFER FUNCTION:
For an LTI system with impulse response h(t), input x(t), and output
y(t) = x(t) * h(t) (convolution), the system's TRANSFER FUNCTION is
defined as the Laplace transform of the impulse response, equivalently
expressed as the ratio of output to input transforms:

        H(s) = Y(s) / X(s) = L{h(t)}

(This follows from the convolution property: Y(s) = X(s) * H(s), see
Section 5.)

CONDITION FOR BIBO STABILITY:
An LTI system (with rational transfer function H(s)) is BIBO stable
if and only if the ROC of H(s) includes the entire j*omega axis
(i.e., Real(s) = 0 must lie within the ROC). This is because BIBO
stability requires absolute integrability of h(t) (Integral of |h(t)|
dt < infinity, see signals_and_systems_basics.txt), which is precisely
the condition for the Laplace integral defining H(s) to converge when
evaluated along s = j*omega (sigma = 0).

CONDITION FOR CAUSALITY:
An LTI system with a RATIONAL transfer function H(s) is CAUSAL if and
only if its ROC is a RIGHT-HALF PLANE located to the right of the
rightmost pole of H(s):

        ROC: Real(s) > sigma_max  (where sigma_max = real part of the
                                     rightmost pole)

This follows directly from Rule 4 in Section 3, since causality
requires h(t) = 0 for t < 0, i.e., h(t) is right-sided.

IMPORTANT COMBINED CASE — CAUSAL AND STABLE:
For a causal LTI system with rational H(s) to ALSO be BIBO stable,
ALL poles of H(s) must lie strictly in the LEFT-HALF of the s-plane
(i.e., every pole must satisfy Real(pole) < 0). This is because the
ROC for a causal system is Real(s) > sigma_max, and for this ROC to
contain the j*omega axis (Real(s)=0), it is required that sigma_max <
0, meaning the rightmost pole — and hence every pole — has negative
real part.

SECTION 5: CORE PROPERTIES OF THE LAPLACE TRANSFORM
-----------------------------------------------

Notation: x(t) <---> X(s), with ROC = R, denotes a Laplace transform
pair and its associated region of convergence.

--- 5.1 LINEARITY ---

        a*x_1(t) + b*x_2(t)  <--->  a*X_1(s) + b*X_2(s)

        ROC: contains the intersection of ROC{x_1} and ROC{x_2}
             (R_1 intersect R_2); the ROC of the sum may be LARGER
             than this intersection if pole-zero cancellation occurs.

--- 5.2 TIME SHIFTING ---

        x(t - t_0)  <--->  exp(-s*t_0) * X(s)

        ROC: unchanged (same as ROC of X(s))

--- 5.3 SHIFTING IN THE s-DOMAIN ---

        exp(s_0*t) * x(t)  <--->  X(s - s_0)

        ROC: shifted version of the original ROC, i.e.,
             ROC{x(t)} shifted by Real(s_0); if original ROC is
             Real(s) > sigma_0, the new ROC is Real(s) > sigma_0 +
             Real(s_0).

--- 5.4 DIFFERENTIATION IN TIME ---

For the BILATERAL transform:

        d/dt [x(t)]  <--->  s * X(s)

        ROC: contains at least R (may be larger if a pole at s=0 is
             cancelled by the differentiation)

For the UNILATERAL transform (incorporating the initial condition,
critical for solving differential equations):

        d/dt [x(t)]  <--->  s * X(s) - x(0-)

where x(0-) denotes the value of x(t) evaluated at t = 0-,
immediately prior to t=0 (this correctly captures any discontinuity
or impulse occurring exactly at the origin). For the second derivative:

        d^2/dt^2 [x(t)]  <--->  s^2 * X(s) - s*x(0-) - x'(0-)

where x'(0-) is the derivative of x(t) evaluated at t=0-.

--- 5.5 INTEGRATION IN TIME ---

For the bilateral transform:

        Integral from -infinity to t of x(tau) d(tau)  <--->
                (1/s) * X(s)

        ROC: contains the intersection of R and the half-plane
             Real(s) > 0 (an additional pole may be introduced at
             s = 0 by the integration operation).

For the unilateral transform (integrating from 0- to t):

        Integral from 0- to t of x(tau) d(tau)  <--->  (1/s) * X(s)

-----------------------------------------------
SECTION 6: COMMON LAPLACE TRANSFORM PAIRS (with ROC)
-----------------------------------------------

(1) Unit Impulse:
        delta(t)   <--->   1
        ROC: entire s-plane

(2) Unit Step:
        u(t)   <--->   1/s
        ROC: Real(s) > 0

(3) Ramp:
        t * u(t)   <--->   1/s^2
        ROC: Real(s) > 0

(4) Real Causal Exponential:
        exp(-a*t) * u(t)   <--->   1 / (s + a)
        ROC: Real(s) > -Real(a)
        (Pole located at s = -a; ROC is the right-half plane to the
        right of this single pole, consistent with Rule 4.)

(5) Real Anti-Causal (Left-Sided) Exponential:
        -exp(-a*t) * u(-t)   <--->   1 / (s + a)
        ROC: Real(s) < -Real(a)
        (Identical algebraic expression for X(s) as pair (4), but a
        DIFFERENT, left-half-plane ROC, demonstrating that X(s) alone
        does NOT uniquely specify x(t) — the ROC must always be
        specified together with X(s).)

(6) Sine Function:
        sin(omega_0*t) * u(t)   <--->   omega_0 / (s^2 + omega_0^2)
        ROC: Real(s) > 0
        (Poles at s = +j*omega_0 and s = -j*omega_0, both purely
        imaginary; the ROC lies to the right of the rightmost pole,
        whose real part is 0.)

(7) Cosine Function:
        cos(omega_0*t) * u(t)   <--->   s / (s^2 + omega_0^2)
        ROC: Real(s) > 0
        (Same pole locations as pair (6): s = +/- j*omega_0.)

(8) Exponentially Damped Sine:
        exp(-a*t) * sin(omega_0*t) * u(t)   <--->
                omega_0 / [ (s+a)^2 + omega_0^2 ]
        ROC: Real(s) > -Real(a)
        (Poles at s = -a +/- j*omega_0.)

(9) Exponentially Damped Cosine:
        exp(-a*t) * cos(omega_0*t) * u(t)   <--->
                (s + a) / [ (s+a)^2 + omega_0^2 ]
        ROC: Real(s) > -Real(a)

(10) t-Weighted Causal Exponential:
        t * exp(-a*t) * u(t)   <--->   1 / (s + a)^2
        ROC: Real(s) > -Real(a)

-----------------------------------------------
SECTION 1: CONTINUOUS-TO-DISCRETE (C/D) CONVERSION VIA IMPULSE-TRAIN SAMPLING
-----------------------------------------------

CONCEPTUAL MODEL:
The standard mathematical idealization of sampling represents the
process as multiplying a continuous-time signal x(t) by a periodic
impulse train p(t), consisting of unit impulses spaced T seconds apart
(T is the SAMPLING PERIOD):

        p(t) = Sum from n = -infinity to infinity of delta(t - n*T)

The resulting IMPULSE-SAMPLED SIGNAL is:

        x_s(t) = x(t) * p(t) = x(t) * Sum over n of delta(t - n*T)
               = Sum from n = -infinity to infinity of
                    x(n*T) * delta(t - n*T)

Each impulse in x_s(t) is weighted by the value of x(t) at the
corresponding sample instant n*T. This impulse-sampled signal x_s(t)
is the standard mathematical model used to derive the frequency-domain
consequences of sampling; the practical conversion to an actual
discrete-time SEQUENCE is then simply:

        x[n] = x(n*T)

i.e., the sequence of weights of the impulses in x_s(t), reindexed as
an ordinary discrete sequence.

THE SAMPLING FREQUENCY:
The (angular) SAMPLING FREQUENCY, corresponding to sampling period T,
is defined as:

        omega_s = 2*pi / T

(with units of radians/second; the ordinary, non-angular sampling
frequency in Hz is f_s = 1/T = omega_s / (2*pi)).

-----------------------------------------------
SECTION 2: MATHEMATICAL DERIVATION OF THE SAMPLED SIGNAL SPECTRUM
-----------------------------------------------

DERIVATION:
Since x_s(t) = x(t) * p(t) is a PRODUCT (multiplication) of two
time-domain signals, the CTFT Multiplication Property states that the
spectrum of a time-domain product equals the (1/(2*pi)-scaled)
CONVOLUTION of the individual spectra in the frequency domain:

        X_s(j*omega) = (1/(2*pi)) * [ X(j*omega) * P(j*omega) ]

where "*" here denotes frequency-domain convolution.

The CTFT of the periodic impulse train p(t) (itself periodic with
period T) is, by the standard CTFT pair for a periodic impulse train,
ALSO a periodic impulse train in the frequency domain:

        P(j*omega) = omega_s * Sum from k = -infinity to infinity of
                      delta(omega - k*omega_s)

Substituting P(j*omega) into the convolution and using the sifting
property of the impulse function under convolution (convolving any
function with a shifted/scaled delta train reproduces shifted/scaled
copies of that function):

        X_s(j*omega) = (1/(2*pi)) * X(j*omega) * 
                        [ omega_s * Sum over k of delta(omega - k*omega_s) ]

              = (omega_s / (2*pi)) * Sum over k of X(j*(omega - k*omega_s))

Since omega_s = 2*pi/T, the prefactor omega_s/(2*pi) simplifies exactly
to 1/T, giving the FINAL RESULT:

        X_s(j*omega) = (1/T) * Sum from k = -infinity to infinity of
                        X(j*(omega - k*omega_s))

INTERPRETATION:
The spectrum of the impulse-sampled signal, X_s(j*omega), consists of
an infinite number of exact (scaled-by-1/T) COPIES of the original
spectrum X(j*omega), each copy shifted by an integer multiple k of the
sampling frequency omega_s, and superimposed (summed) together. This
periodic replication of the spectrum is the central mathematical fact
underlying all sampling theory.

-----------------------------------------------
SECTION 3: THE NYQUIST SAMPLING THEOREM
-----------------------------------------------

SETUP — BAND-LIMITED SIGNALS:
Let x(t) be BAND-LIMITED, meaning its CTFT satisfies:

        X(j*omega) = 0      for all |omega| > omega_m

where omega_m is the highest (maximum) frequency component present in
x(t).

STATEMENT OF THE THEOREM:
Given the spectral replication result of Section 2, the shifted copies
of X(j*omega) in X_s(j*omega) are centered at omega = 0, +/- omega_s,
+/- 2*omega_s, and so on, with EACH COPY occupying the frequency range
[-omega_m, +omega_m] around its center. To avoid these adjacent copies
OVERLAPPING with one another (an effect called ALIASING, discussed in
Section 4), the spacing between adjacent copies (which is exactly
omega_s) must be large enough that the copy centered at 0 (occupying
up to +omega_m) does not overlap with the copy centered at omega_s
(occupying down to omega_s - omega_m). This non-overlap condition
requires:

        omega_m < omega_s - omega_m
   =>   omega_s > 2 * omega_m

THE NYQUIST SAMPLING THEOREM therefore states: a band-limited
continuous-time signal x(t), with no frequency content at or above
omega_m, is UNIQUELY AND EXACTLY RECOVERABLE from its samples x(nT) if
and only if the sampling frequency satisfies the STRICT inequality:

        omega_s > 2 * omega_m

NYQUIST RATE DEFINITION:
The quantity 2*omega_m (exactly twice the maximum frequency component
of the band-limited signal) is defined as the NYQUIST RATE:

        Nyquist Rate = 2 * omega_m

Sampling at exactly the Nyquist rate (omega_s = 2*omega_m, satisfying
equality rather than strict inequality) is generally INSUFFICIENT for
guaranteed perfect reconstruction in the general case (e.g., a pure
cosine at exactly omega_m sampled at exactly omega_s = 2*omega_m can
alias to zero, depending on the sampling phase), which is precisely
why the theorem's sufficient condition for guaranteed reconstruction
is stated as the strict inequality omega_s > 2*omega_m.

-----------------------------------------------
SECTION 4: ALIASING
-----------------------------------------------

DEFINITION AND MECHANISM:
ALIASING occurs whenever the Nyquist condition is violated, i.e.,
whenever:

        omega_s <= 2 * omega_m

In this case, the spectral copies in X_s(j*omega) = (1/T) * Sum over k
of X(j*(omega - k*omega_s)) are spaced too closely together (by
omega_s) relative to their width (2*omega_m), so ADJACENT REPLICAS
OVERLAP AND ADD TOGETHER in the overlapping frequency regions.

MECHANICAL CONSEQUENCE:
Once overlap occurs, the high-frequency content of x(t) (frequency
components near and above omega_s/2) gets folded back and superimposed
on top of, and becomes indistinguishable from, genuine LOWER-FREQUENCY
content within the baseband region. Specifically, a true frequency
component at omega_0 (where omega_s/2 < omega_0 <= omega_s) will, after
sampling, appear (alias) at the lower apparent frequency:

        omega_alias = omega_s - omega_0     (for omega_0 in the range
                                                described above, folding
                                                about omega_s/2)

More generally, any original frequency component "masquerades" as a
different, lower frequency that is indistinguishable, after sampling,
from a genuinely low-frequency signal at that aliased frequency.

DESTRUCTIVE, IRREVERSIBLE NATURE OF ALIASING:
Once the overlapping copies have summed together in the sampled
spectrum, the original, unaliased spectral content CANNOT be recovered
by any subsequent processing (such as filtering) applied to the
samples — the overlap has irreversibly corrupted/destroyed the
original information in the overlapping frequency band. This is why
anti-aliasing analog low-pass filters are placed before the sampler in
practical systems: to forcibly band-limit x(t) (removing or
attenuating content above omega_s/2) BEFORE sampling occurs, since
aliasing cannot be undone after the fact.

-----------------------------------------------
SECTION 5: IDEAL SIGNAL RECONSTRUCTION
-----------------------------------------------

ROLE OF THE IDEAL LOW-PASS FILTER:
Provided the Nyquist condition omega_s > 2*omega_m is satisfied, the
copies of X(j*omega) in X_s(j*omega) are non-overlapping, and the
original spectrum X(j*omega) — and hence x(t) itself — can be EXACTLY
recovered from the samples by passing the impulse-sampled signal
x_s(t) through an IDEAL LOW-PASS FILTER H_r(j*omega) with the
following exact specifications:

        H_r(j*omega) = T          for |omega| < omega_s / 2
        H_r(j*omega) = 0          for |omega| > omega_s / 2

   - CUTOFF FREQUENCY: omega_s / 2 (also called the "folding
     frequency" or "Nyquist frequency" of the sampling system); this
     cutoff must be chosen to pass the entire k=0 copy of X(j*omega)
     (which occupies up to omega_m, and omega_m < omega_s/2 is
     guaranteed by the Nyquist condition) while rejecting/blocking all
     other (k != 0) shifted copies.
   - PASSBAND GAIN: exactly T (the sampling period); this specific
     gain value is required to exactly cancel the 1/T scaling factor
     present in X_s(j*omega) (derived in Section 2), so that the
     filter output spectrum exactly equals X(j*omega), and hence the
     filter's time-domain output exactly equals the original x(t).

TIME-DOMAIN RECONSTRUCTION — WHITTAKER-SHANNON INTERPOLATION:
The impulse response of the ideal reconstruction low-pass filter
described above, h_r(t), is (by the standard inverse-CTFT of an ideal
low-pass filter) a SINC FUNCTION:

        h_r(t) = sinc(t / T)

(using the convention sinc(x) = sin(pi*x) / (pi*x), which satisfies
sinc(0) = 1 and sinc(integer) = 0 for any nonzero integer).

Since the reconstruction filter's output x(t) is obtained by
convolving the impulse-sampled signal x_s(t) = Sum over n of x(nT) *
delta(t - nT) with this impulse response h_r(t), and convolution with
a delta function simply shifts and reproduces the impulse response, the
result is the WHITTAKER-SHANNON INTERPOLATION FORMULA:

        x(t) = Sum from n = -infinity to infinity of
               x(n*T) * sinc( (t - n*T) / T )

INTERPRETATION:
This formula exactly reconstructs the continuous-time signal x(t), for
ALL real-valued t (not just at the sample points), as a weighted sum
of time-shifted sinc functions, where each sinc function is centered
exactly at a sample instant n*T and weighted by the corresponding
sample value x(nT). This is the precise mathematical sense in which a
properly band-limited, properly sampled (Nyquist-satisfying) signal is
said to be "perfectly reconstructible" from its discrete samples alone.
